The science behind

We model daily lawn water need from temperature using a smooth curve fit to DIN-18035-2 guidance for turf: >30 °C -> >5 mm/day, 25-30 °C -> 3-4 mm/day, 20-25 °C -> 2-3 mm/day, 15-20 °C -> <2 mm/day. These values refer to multi-hour daily maximum temperatures and are moderated by humidity and air movement.

In practice, irrigation demand tracks evapotranspiration (ET), the combined evaporation from soil and transpiration from plants. ET is driven mainly by temperature, solar radiation, wind, and humidity, and is often estimated from weather station data.

Soil texture matters because it controls water-holding capacity and infiltration rate: sandy soils hold less water but infiltrate quickly, while finer soils hold more water but infiltrate more slowly, increasing runoff risk. Turf species also matter; typical daily water use ranges are ~3-8 mm for cool-season grasses and ~2-5 mm for warm-season grasses under non-limiting moisture conditions.

The parameters below control the curve:

  • Min (mm) sets the baseline water need at low temperatures.
  • Max (mm) sets the upper bound at high temperatures.
  • g controls how quickly the curve rises.
  • m shifts the midpoint (temperature where the curve bends).

The red point marks the recent 90th-percentile temperature (last 24 hours) mapped onto your custom curve. As dynamic irrigation evolves, we'll combine this curve with weather inputs and site factors like soil, humidity, wind, shading, and irrigation efficiency.
